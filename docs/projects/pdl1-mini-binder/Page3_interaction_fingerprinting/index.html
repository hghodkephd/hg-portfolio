Part 3: Structure & Interface Analysis

31 designs made it through the filter. Where do they land on PD-L1?

Previously, in Part 2: We swept three parameters — binder length, hotspot configuration, and noise scale — across 480 backbones and found that 31 designs pass structural filtering (pLDDT ≥ 70, RMSD ≤ 1.5 Å). Cluster A and 70-mers were the most productive conditions; Cluster B showed the highest pass rate but the fewest geometry-passing backbones. Read Part 2 →

That study told us which conditions work. This page asks the next question: do the 31 passing designs actually engage PD-L1 in a way that makes biological sense?

Page 1 — Pipeline overview Page 2 — Setup & parameter sensitivity Page 3 — Structure & interface analysis Page 4 — Energy minimization & MD

What we found

  • All three hotspot configurations converge on the same PD-L1 surface patch (residues 119–121) — the designs are not landing randomly.
  • Hotspot coverage is partial: 40.6% for Cluster A, 23.4% for Distributed, 11.4% for Cluster B. Guidance biases backbone placement, but sidechains bridge the last few angstroms.
  • About half of hotspot contacts recover native PD-1 chemistry (same amino acid or same class), and the interaction profile is substantially more native-like than expected from de novo designs.
  • These results are reliable for triage and library design — not for energetics, which require MD (Part 4).

First, let's look at the structures

Before any quantitative analysis, let's just look. Load a few designs from different hotspot configurations and see what jumps out. Do the binders sit flush against the PD-L1 surface, or are they detached? Do different configurations approach different parts of the target? Do the backbones look like compact helical bundles — or do any of them look collapsed or extended in ways that don't make physical sense?

Interaction fingerprinting

The viewer gives us a qualitative feel. Now let's get quantitative. Interaction fingerprinting maps every residue-level contact between each passing binder and PD-L1, classifies them by chemistry, and compares the result to the native PD-1/PD-L1 interface (crystal structure 4ZQK). It answers the question: are these designs engaging PD-L1 in a way that resembles a real protein–protein interaction?

The answer depends on which atoms you include. The pipeline produces several representations of each design, and not all of them are appropriate for contact analysis. The table below lays out the distinctions — the short version is that fingerprinting should be performed on full-atom representations (all heavy atoms on both binder and target), not on backbone-only structures where sidechain-mediated contacts are invisible.

Know your representations

Name Binder atoms Target atoms Good for
RFdiffusion complex pose Backbone only (poly-Gly) Full-atom Topology, docking mode
Threaded complex (viewer) Backbone only (designed seq.) Full-atom Visual inspection
ESMFold binder Full-atom (sidechains) — Sidechain geometry, single chain
Superimposed ESMFold-on-complex Full-atom (sidechains) Full-atom Full contact analysis ✓

For the analysis below, we superimposed each ESMFold-predicted binder (full sidechains) onto the RFdiffusion docked geometry via Cα alignment (RMSD 0.24–1.46 Å, median 0.67 Å), giving a two-chain complex with all heavy atoms on both sides. This is the correct setup for a standard 4.5 Å heavy-atom contact cutoff. Across 31 designs, the analysis found 499 binder–target contact pairs, 76 of which occur at specified hotspot positions.

One caveat, stated once

These results are reliable for topology and triage — identifying which surface regions are engaged and which designs deserve further investment. They are not reliable for energetics: sidechain rotamers are unoptimized, contact types are inferred from residue identity, and no force field has been applied. Energetic interpretation requires MD (Part 4).

Where do the designs actually land on PD-L1?

Regardless of which hotspot residues were specified, where does RFdiffusion place the binder? Let's start with the contact frequency across PD-L1 residues. Figure 2 makes the answer clear: the designs are not landing randomly.

Figure 2. Contact frequency by PD-L1 residue, full-sidechain analysis. For each residue on PD-L1 (x-axis), bars show the fraction of passing designs per hotspot configuration that make at least one heavy-atom contact (≤ 4.5 Å). n = 16, 7, and 8 designs for Cluster A, B, and Distributed respectively.

Residues 119–121 are a convergent docking region. Residue 120 is contacted by 94% of Cluster A designs, 100% of Cluster B, and 100% of Distributed. Residues 119 and 121 track the same pattern. None are specified hotspots in any condition — the model finds this patch on its own. That's a good sign: all three hotspot configurations converge on the same preferred PD-L1 surface, which overlaps with the edge of the native PD-1 binding footprint.

Did the designs contact their specified hotspots?

Let's test whether the hotspot guidance actually worked. For each design, what fraction of its specified hotspot residues are contacted?

Coverage is partial but real. Cluster A reaches 40.6%, Distributed 23.4%, Cluster B 11.4%. The pattern makes sense: coverage is highest where the specified hotspots overlap with the convergent 119–121 docking region (Cluster A includes residue 121), and lowest where specified residues sit far from it (Cluster B). Hotspot guidance biases where the backbone lands, but sidechains are what actually bridge the last few angstroms to the target.

Are the designs reinventing PD-1, or finding something new?

At each hotspot contact, we compare the designed residue to the native PD-1 residue at the same PD-L1 position: exact match (same amino acid), same class (same physicochemical group), or novel (different class). See the Appendix for the classification logic.

Figure 4. PD-1 mimicry at hotspot positions. Left: overall distribution across 76 full-sidechain hotspot contacts. Right: breakdown by hotspot configuration. Cluster B contributes only 4 contacts — too few for a reliable percentage. Native PD-1 contacts drawn from 4ZQK.

About half novel, half native-like — a meaningful sanity check. 48.7% of hotspot contacts are novel (different physicochemical class from PD-1), 40.8% are same-class, and 10.5% are exact amino acid matches (8 contacts, concentrated in Cluster A and Distributed). The backbone-only first pass had reported 89.6% novel — which made the designs look like they were ignoring native chemistry entirely. With full sidechains, the picture flips: ProteinMPNN is placing residues that recapitulate PD-1-like interactions wherever the backbone geometry supports it. It's not generating random sequences at the interface.

What kind of chemistry is happening at the interface?

We classify each contact by the physicochemical classes of the two residues involved. These are potential interaction types inferred from residue identity — not geometrically confirmed. See the Appendix for classification rules.

Figure 5. Interaction type distribution by configuration (full-sidechain analysis). Native PD-1 contacts (4ZQK) shown for reference. Interaction types are inferred from residue physicochemistry, not atomic geometry — see Appendix.

The interaction profile is much closer to native than it first appeared. H-bonds: 7.5–10.1% (up from 0–7% backbone-only), salt bridges: 10.1–15.0% (up from 0–1%), mixed contacts: 42–46% (down from 55–78%, approaching native PD-1's 39%). A hydrogen bond gap persists — native PD-1 uses 19.4% — consistent with ProteinMPNN's known preference for Ala-rich sequences. Interestingly, the designs now exceed native salt bridge frequency (10–15% vs 6.5%). That could be real or could be an ESMFold rotamer artifact. MD will tell.

From fingerprinting to experimental strategy

This analysis is not an end in itself. The real payoff is what it tells you about how to design the next experiment.

The clearest application is combinatorial library design. Modern screening libraries use defined triplet codons to restrict each position to specific amino acid subsets. The fingerprint tells you which subsets to choose. Positions that consistently make hydrophobic contacts get restricted to Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Phe. Positions where a hydrogen-bond donor matters get restricted to Asn, Gln, Ser, Thr. This is chemically informed diversity: you trade sequence complexity for higher odds of hitting functional chemistry.

Quantifying the reduction: a fully randomised 6-position library encodes 206 = 64 million variants. Restricting each position to 4–5 residues based on the fingerprint drops the theoretical space to ~15,000–80,000 variants. That still exceeds plate-based capacity (~103–104), but it is tractable for phage or yeast display — and the resulting hits are more likely to be chemically meaningful. The fingerprint doesn't replace screening; it makes the search space smaller and more principled.

There's an additional lever. Where the H-bond gap is the concern, you can deliberately encode polar and charged residues at interface positions — compensating for ProteinMPNN's Ala bias with wet-lab diversity, and filling in the interaction chemistry the model under-represents.

Bottom line

Interface fingerprinting moved this project from structural filtering to experimental prioritisation. The designs converge on a preferred PD-L1 surface, partially recover specified hotspots, and show a more native-like interaction profile than the first-pass analysis indicated. None of this proves binding. What it does is turn generative design outputs into experimentally testable hypotheses — and identify which designs and interface positions are worth the investment of molecular dynamics and wet-lab library construction.

What's next: energy minimization and molecular dynamics

The fingerprinting results are a starting point, not an endpoint. The ESMFold structures are unoptimized — sidechain rotamers are predictions, not physically relaxed conformations. A contact we classify as a hydrogen bond may have the wrong donor–acceptor geometry. A salt bridge may be a clash. The numbers are reliable for topology and triage; they are not reliable for energetic interpretation.

What Part 4 adds

Energy minimization resolves steric clashes and settles sidechains into realistic rotameric states — finding the nearest local energy minimum from the ESMFold starting point. Molecular dynamics goes further: it runs the structure in explicit solvent at physiological temperature, letting contacts that are truly stable persist and transient ones dissolve. After MD, the fingerprinting analysis can be re-run on equilibrated structures, and the interaction classifications become physically meaningful.

Part 4 applies both steps to the top-ranked designs identified here. The fingerprinting results from this page define the pre-MD baseline and determine which designs are worth the GPU investment.

Appendix: definitions & methodology

The metrics on this page are easy to miscommunicate. This section defines every term, specifies the atomic representations used, and provides explicit formulae.

Atomic representation

Which atoms are present on each chain?

Target (PD-L1, chain A): Full-atom representation from the RFdiffusion complex pose PDB. All backbone atoms (N, CA, C, O) and all sidechain heavy atoms are present. Hydrogens are excluded.

Binder (chain B) — backbone-only analysis: The original notebook 07 analysis used the RFdiffusion complex pose, where the binder chain is poly-glycine. Only backbone heavy atoms (N, CA, C, O) are present. Glycine has no sidechain beyond H atoms.

Binder (chain B) — full-sidechain analysis: ESMFold-predicted binder structures (single-chain, full heavy atoms including all sidechain atoms) are superimposed onto the binder backbone of the RFdiffusion complex pose via Cα least-squares alignment. The superimposed ESMFold binder is then paired with the PD-L1 target chain from the complex pose. This gives a two-chain complex with full heavy atoms on both sides, in the docked geometry.

Contact definition

Two residues are in contact if any heavy atom (non-hydrogen) on residue i of the binder is within d ≤ 4.5 Å of any heavy atom on residue j of the target.

contact(i, j) = 1  if  mina∈atoms(i), b∈atoms(j) ‖a − b‖ ≤ 4.5 Å
contact(i, j) = 0  otherwise

Each binder–target residue pair is counted once regardless of how many atom pairs satisfy the distance criterion. The 4.5 Å cutoff is a standard heavy-atom contact threshold in structural biology (see Venkatakrishnan et al., Nature 2013).

Contact frequency

For a given PD-L1 residue j and hotspot configuration c:

contact_frequency(j, c) = (number of designs in config c that contact residue j) / (total designs in config c) × 100%

A design "contacts" residue j if any of its binder residues is in contact with j (as defined above).

Hotspot coverage

For a single design d with specified hotspot set Hc:

coverage(d) = |{j ∈ Hc : ∃ binder residue i such that contact(i, j) = 1}| / |Hc| × 100%

In plain language: what fraction of the design's specified hotspot residues are actually contacted by at least one binder residue? The hotspot sets are: Cluster A = {54, 56, 121, 123}, Cluster B = {26, 56, 113, 123, 125}, Distributed = {19, 20, 54, 56, 66, 117, 121, 123}.

Mean coverage per configuration is the arithmetic mean of coverage(d) across all designs in that configuration.

PD-1 mimicry

At each hotspot position j where a designed binder makes contact, the designed binder residue's amino acid is compared to the native PD-1 residue that contacts the same PD-L1 position in crystal structure 4ZQK:

Exact match: same amino acid identity (e.g., both Asn at PD-L1 Asp122).

Same class: different amino acid, same physicochemical class (hydrophobic, aromatic, polar, or charged — see classification table below).

Novel: different physicochemical class from native PD-1.

This is assessed per contact pair, not per design. A single design can contribute multiple mimicry classifications at different hotspot positions.

Interaction type classification

Each contacting residue is assigned a physicochemical class based on its amino acid identity:

Class Amino acids
Hydrophobic Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Met, Pro, Gly
Aromatic Phe, Trp, Tyr
Polar Ser, Thr, Asn, Gln, Cys
Charged Asp, Glu, Lys, Arg, His

The interaction type for a binder–target contact pair is then determined by the combination of the two residue classes:

Interaction type Residue class pair
Salt bridge charged + charged
Hydrogen bond polar + polar, or polar + charged
Hydrophobic hydrophobic + hydrophobic, hydrophobic + aromatic, or aromatic + aromatic
Mixed any other combination (e.g., polar + hydrophobic, charged + aromatic)

Important caveat: this classification is based on residue identity, not on the actual geometry of the interaction. A residue pair classified as "hydrogen bond" means the physicochemical classes are compatible with H-bonding — it does not mean the donor–acceptor geometry is correct. True H-bond validation requires energy minimization and geometric criteria (distance ≤ 3.5 Å, angle ≥ 120°). These are potential interaction types, not validated ones.

Superposition method

ESMFold binder structures are aligned to the RFdiffusion complex binder backbone using least-squares Cα superposition (Kabsch algorithm). The transformation that minimizes:

RMSD = √( (1/N) Σk=1..N ‖CAkcomplex − R·CAkesmfold − t‖² )

is applied to all atoms of the ESMFold binder (not just Cα). Alignment RMSDs ranged from 0.24 Å to 1.46 Å across the 31 designs (median 0.67 Å), confirming that ESMFold closely reproduces the RFdiffusion backbone geometry. Two designs with RMSD > 1.2 Å (ranks 28 and 31) should be interpreted with caution — the superimposed sidechain positions are less reliable at higher backbone deviation.
