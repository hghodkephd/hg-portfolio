Interaction fingerprinting

The parameter study told us which conditions produce structurally valid designs. The viewer above shows what they look like. Now we ask the harder question: how do these designs engage the target?

The fingerprinting analysis maps every residue-level contact (≤ 4.5 Å heavy-atom distance) between each of the 31 passing binders and PD-L1, classifies each contact by residue physicochemistry, and compares the results to the native PD-1/PD-L1 interface from crystal structure 4ZQK. Across 31 designs, the analysis found 161 total binder–target contact pairs, of which 48 occur at known hotspot positions where a direct comparison to PD-1 is possible.

Read before interpreting — these structures are not energy-minimized

The full pipeline is: RFdiffusion → ProteinMPNN → ESMFold. ESMFold produces a full-atom model with real sidechains, but in a single forward pass — no force-field relaxation has been applied. In practical terms, this means sidechain rotamers are ESMFold's best guess, not physically optimized conformations. A contact classified as a hydrogen bond may have the wrong donor–acceptor angle. A hydrophobic contact may be a steric clash in reality. An "exact match" to native PD-1 is not meaningful if the sidechain is pointing the wrong way.

The reliable signal here is topological: which regions of the PD-L1 surface are being approached, and how consistently. For chemically accurate contact descriptions, these structures need energy minimization followed by short molecular dynamics equilibration — that is exactly what the next installment (Part 4) addresses. We run fingerprinting first because it is CPU-only and takes minutes, while MD requires GPU time per structure. The results here inform which designs are worth that investment.

Which PD-L1 residues are contacted?

The first question is topological: regardless of what hotspot residues we told RFdiffusion to target, which PD-L1 residues did the designs actually approach? The chart below shows the fraction of passing designs in each hotspot configuration that make contact at each PD-L1 residue position.

Residues 120 and 121 are a convergent docking site. PD-L1 Gly120 was approached by 87.5% of Cluster A, 71.4% of Cluster B, and 87.5% of Distributed designs. Residue 121 was contacted by 62.5%, 71.4%, and 100% respectively — the most consistently contacted position across all conditions. Neither residue is a specified hotspot in any condition. This convergence suggests that RFdiffusion is finding a genuine low-energy binding mode on the PD-L1 surface, independent of the hotspot residues it was instructed to target. In the native crystal structure, this region sits at the edge of the PD-1 binding interface — the model appears to be rediscovering it from scratch.

Cluster B specified hotspots: 0% coverage. The five Cluster B hotspot residues (Asp26, Tyr56, Arg113, Tyr123, Arg125) are aromatic/charged residues in the central and C-terminal region of the PD-L1 interface. None of the 7 passing Cluster B designs contacted any of them. Those designs do contact PD-L1 — primarily at residues 61 (85.7%), 120 (71.4%), and 121 (71.4%) — but at positions entirely outside their specified set. The Cluster B residues may define a binding geometry that is difficult for RFdiffusion to satisfy with a compact backbone, or the specified residues may be geometrically incompatible with a single contiguous binding surface.

How well are the specified hotspots covered?

For each design, we ask: of the hotspot residues that were explicitly given to RFdiffusion for that condition, what fraction were actually contacted by the designed binder? This is the most direct measure of whether the model obeyed the design specification.

Mean coverage by config

Per-design coverage

Overall coverage is low: 17.3% ± 15.4%. Cluster A achieves 26.6% mean coverage (its 4 hotspots include Gly120, which sits in the convergent docking zone). Cluster B is 0%. Distributed covers 14.1% despite specifying 8 residues. This is a real finding about the limits of hotspot-guided diffusion: specifying target residues biases backbone placement, but does not guarantee contact — especially when the specified residues span a geometry that no single compact binder can reach simultaneously.

Do the designs mimic PD-1, or find new solutions?

At each hotspot position where a designed binder makes contact, we compare the designed residue to the native PD-1 residue at the same position. Three outcomes are possible: exact match (same amino acid), same class (different amino acid but same physicochemical class — e.g., both hydrophobic), or novel solution (different class entirely). This tells us whether the model is rediscovering known biology or exploring new chemical space.

Overall mimicry (48 contacts)

Mimicry by hotspot config

89.6% novel solutions — expected, but not trivial. De novo binders are not constrained to use native-like chemistry, so a high novel fraction is expected. What makes this interesting is the 2 exact matches: both occur at Asp122 on PD-L1, where the designed binder places an Asn — exactly what native PD-1 does at that position. Asp–Asn is one of the most conserved hydrogen-bonding pairs in PPI interfaces. The fact that ProteinMPNN independently converges on this solution, at this position, is a meaningful sanity check: the sequence design model is recovering known biophysical logic where the geometry supports it.

The 3 same-class matches (6.3%) also cluster at positions where the native PD-1 contact is hydrophobic — the easiest class to substitute within. The remaining 89.6% novel solutions are not a failure; they are the expected outcome when a de novo backbone approaches the target from a different angle than PD-1. Novel does not mean wrong — it means the contact has not been validated against the native reference.

What types of interactions are being made?

Contact type classification is based on the physicochemical classes of the two contacting residues: a charged–charged pair is classified as a potential salt bridge, a polar–charged pair as a potential hydrogen bond, two hydrophobic residues as a hydrophobic contact, and so on. Mixed contacts involve residues from different classes where no specific directional interaction is expected.

The hydrogen bond gap is the clearest signal in this analysis. Native PD-1 uses 20% hydrogen bonds and 6.5% salt bridges — directional, specific interactions that define binding affinity and selectivity. The designed binders use 0–7% hydrogen bonds and are dominated by "mixed" contacts (55–78%). This pattern is consistent across all three hotspot configurations. Hydrogen bonds require precise donor–acceptor geometry that is unlikely to emerge from unoptimized structures, so some of this gap may close after energy minimization. But it also reflects a real tendency of ProteinMPNN: the model favors Ala-rich sequences (visible in the designed sequences above), which limits the chemical repertoire available for directional contacts.

The dominance of hydrophobic contacts in Distributed designs (42.4%) is worth noting — it suggests those binders are packing against PD-L1 via nonspecific surface complementarity rather than specific polar interactions. This is not inherently bad (many natural PPIs have significant hydrophobic character), but it does raise the question of specificity: a binder that relies entirely on shape complementarity may bind promiscuously.

Why interaction types matter beyond the model

This analysis is not just a validation exercise. Understanding which types of interactions the model favors — and which it fails to produce — has direct implications for how you would translate these designs into an experimental campaign.

Consider ultra-high-throughput screening (uHTS) library design. Modern combinatorial libraries use defined triplet codons to restrict each position to specific subsets of amino acids. If the fingerprinting analysis tells you that positions X, Y, and Z on the binder consistently make hydrophobic contacts with the target, you can restrict those positions to hydrophobic residues (Ala, Val, Leu, Ile, Phe) rather than encoding all 20 amino acids. Conversely, if position W requires a hydrogen-bond donor (as the Asn at Asp122 suggests), you restrict to Asn, Gln, Ser, Thr.

The practical payoff: a fully randomized 6-position library with all 20 amino acids encodes 206 = 64 million variants. If fingerprinting narrows each position to 4–5 residues, the library drops to ~15,000–80,000 variants — screening that in a well-plate format instead of requiring phage display. Lower diversity, higher fidelity, higher hit rates. The interaction fingerprint is what tells you which positions to restrict and which residue classes to allow.

This is where the hydrogen bond gap becomes actionable. If the computational designs are under-representing directional interactions, you can deliberately encode polar and charged residues at interface positions in the screening library — compensating for the model's Ala bias with wet-lab diversity. The fingerprinting analysis does not just validate the pipeline; it informs the next experimental step.

What's next: why these structures need molecular dynamics

Throughout this analysis, we have flagged a critical limitation: none of these structures have been energy-minimized. This is worth unpacking, because it defines the boundary between what the current analysis can and cannot tell us.

A protein structure at the atomic level is described by the positions of every atom. In a real protein, those positions settle into a local energy minimum — a configuration where the forces on every atom are approximately balanced. Bond lengths are correct, van der Waals contacts are favorable, hydrogen bonds have the right geometry, and there are no steric clashes (atoms overlapping).

ESMFold does not produce structures at an energy minimum. It predicts a plausible fold from the amino acid sequence using a learned potential, but it does not apply a physics-based force field. The result is a structure that looks right at the backbone level (hence the high pLDDT scores) but may have problems at the atomic level: sidechain rotamers that would be strained, contacts that are too close or too far, hydrogen bonds where the donor and acceptor are misaligned.

What energy minimization and MD simulation do

Energy minimization takes the ESMFold structure and iteratively adjusts atomic positions to reduce the total potential energy — resolving steric clashes, optimizing bond angles, and settling sidechains into realistic rotameric states. This is a gradient descent on the potential energy surface: it finds the nearest local minimum, not a global one.

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation goes further: it adds kinetic energy (temperature) and solvent (explicit water molecules), then integrates Newton's equations of motion over time. A short MD run (10–50 ns) lets the structure breathe — flexible loops move, sidechains repack, and contacts that are truly stable persist while transient ones disappear. After MD, the fingerprinting analysis can be re-run on the equilibrated structures, and the interaction type classifications become physically meaningful.

The next installment (Part 4) applies both steps to the top-ranked designs identified here: energy minimization to fix the atomic-level geometry, followed by short MD trajectories to assess which binder–target contacts are stable under thermal motion. The fingerprinting results from this page serve as the pre-MD baseline — and the comparison between pre- and post-MD contacts will reveal which of these predicted interactions are real.
